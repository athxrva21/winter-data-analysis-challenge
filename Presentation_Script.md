### Slide 1 — Title *(0:00–0:14)*
Is Australia doing well? The tempting answer is to build one number and rank it. So we did — and then we spent the whole project trying to break our own verdict.

### Slide 2 — The question *(0:14–0:36)*
On the OECD's well-being data, Australia comes fourteenth of thirty-eight. **[CLICK]** But a single number is the least honest way to answer a vague question. So we refined it: is that rank robust — to how we measure, to which dimensions we count, and to which way the country is moving?

### Slide 3 — Finding 1 *(0:36–0:58)*
First, decompose it. **[CLICK]** Standardised against the OECD, Australia is best in the club on voter turnout, top-ten on income and life expectancy. **[CLICK]** And at the same time, bottom-quartile on long hours, negative emotions and heat. Averaging those doesn't summarise Australia — it erases it.

### Slide 4 — Finding 2 *(0:58–1:26)*
But is even that fourteenth real? We stress-tested it. **[CLICK]** A three-thousand-run Monte-Carlo keeps Australia above the OECD median in eighty-nine percent of worlds — **[CLICK]** but in the top third only forty-four. "Above average" is a finding; "among the best" is a recipe choice. **[CLICK]** The single biggest lever? Compulsory voting — remove it, and Australia drops eight places.

### Slide 5 — Finding 3 *(1:26–1:50)*
A rank is a snapshot; direction matters more. Testing every trend with Mann-Kendall and Theil–Sen — built from scratch — the country splits, and the split is statistically real. **[CLICK]** Improving: income, jobs, life expectancy, even falling work hours. **[CLICK]** Worsening: social support, and deaths from suicide, alcohol and drugs. Richer at work; lonelier at heart.

### Slide 6 — Finding 4 *(1:50–2:14)*
The obvious rebuttal — maybe every rich country is getting lonelier. **[CLICK]** It fails. Australia's rise in loneliness, and in deaths of despair, each outpaced roughly eighty-nine percent of the OECD — while the OECD median actually fell. On its weakest dimensions, Australia is moving against the pack.

### Slide 7 — Finding 5 *(2:14–2:36)*
And our data has a blind spot — the most damning one. **[CLICK]** Life satisfaction goes dark after 2020, exactly where we found trouble. So we filled it with the World Happiness Report: **[CLICK]** Australians' own life ratings are at a record-low six-point-nine-seven, eleventh in the world. A different survey — the same verdict.

### Slide 8 — The verdict *(2:36–3:00)*
So — is Australia doing well? Above average, and quietly sliding on the things hardest to count. **[CLICK]** Strong on income, jobs and longevity; **[CLICK]** going backwards on connection and distress. In 2023 the Treasury built Australia's first national well-being framework on exactly this premise. The data don't just answer the question — they say where to point. Not the income line, which is fine. At connection, and distress.
