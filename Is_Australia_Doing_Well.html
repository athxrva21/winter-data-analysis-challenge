Is Australia Doing Well?

Team O for 4 — Atharva Aher (sid 55088718) and Mokshithh Kethari (sid 550809791)

One country, three verdicts — a stress-tested well-being audit, 2010–2025

We build a single well-being score for Australia — it ranks 14th of 38 OECD countries — and then stress-test it to destruction. The verdict that survives is "above average"; the one that does not is "among the best." Beneath the average sits the real story: on the human dimensions of well-being, Australia is declining faster than almost the entire OECD.

Refined question: Australia looks above-average on a composite score — is that robust to how we measure, to which dimensions we count, and to which way the country is moving? And where it slips, is it sharing an OECD trend or standing out as an outlier?

Data: OECD How's Life? Well-being Database (21 indicators, 11 domains, 47 countries), plus one external series (World Happiness Report). Dependencies: pandas, numpy, matplotlib — all statistics implemented from scratch.

1. Data and cleaning

The OECD How's Life? database frames well-being as 11 dimensions across material conditions, quality of life and community; our extract carries 21 national-average indicators. Cleaning is deliberate: the raw SDMX export duplicates every field and leaves the human-readable value column empty, so we use the machine columns; we orient every indicator (+1 higher-is-better, −1 lower-is-better) from the OECD metadata; we separate OECD members from partner economies; we keep quality flags rather than dropping estimated points; and we never impute the four indicators Australia simply does not report.

def Phi(z): return 0.5*(1+math.erf(z/math.sqrt(2)))
def mann_kendall(y):
    y=np.asarray(y,float); n=len(y)
    s=sum(np.sign(y[j]-y[i]) for i in range(n-1) for j in range(i+1,n))
    var=n*(n-1)*(2*n+5)/18
    z=(s-1)/math.sqrt(var) if s>0 else (s+1)/math.sqrt(var) if s<0 else 0.0
    return s,z,2*(1-Phi(abs(z)))
def theil_sen(x,y):
    x=np.asarray(x,float); y=np.asarray(y,float)
    sl=[(y[j]-y[i])/(x[j]-x[i]) for i in range(len(x)) for j in range(i+1,len(x)) if x[j]!=x[i]]
    return float(np.median(sl))

def latest(g, lo=2021, hi=2024):
    g=g[(g.year>=lo)&(g.year<=hi)].sort_values('year'); return g.value.iloc[-1] if len(g) else np.nan
oecd=df[df.oecd]
cs=(oecd.groupby(['ind','indicator','domain','direction','country']).apply(latest)
        .rename('value').reset_index().dropna(subset=['value']))
def zc(g):
    g=g.copy(); g['z']=(g.value-g.value.mean())/g.value.std(ddof=0)*g.direction.iloc[0]
    g['rank']=g.value.rank(ascending=(g.direction.iloc[0]<0)); g['n']=len(g); return g
cs=cs.groupby('ind',group_keys=False).apply(zc)

2. Finding 1 — Australia is not one thing

Standardised against the OECD, Australia is best in the club on voter turnout and top-ten on income and life expectancy, yet bottom-quartile on long hours, negative emotions and heat exposure. Averaging these into one number does not summarise Australia — it erases it.

3. Finding 2 — only "above average" is robust

The composite (mean of domain z-scores) ranks Australia 14th of 38, behind every Nordic and behind New Zealand. A 3,000-run Monte-Carlo stress test — adding measurement noise and bootstrap-resampling the indicators — keeps Australia above the OECD median in 89% of perturbed worlds but in the top third only 44% of the time, with a 90% rank interval of roughly 7th–22nd. "Above average" is a finding; "among the best" is a recipe choice. The biggest single lever is compulsory voting: remove the civic-engagement domain and Australia drops eight places.

4. Finding 3 — a two-speed nation

Direction matters more than a snapshot. Trends tested with Mann-Kendall and sized with Theil–Sen (both from scratch): 7 of 13 are significant. Significantly improving — income, employment, life expectancy, and falling work hours and gender wage gap. Significantly worsening — lack of social support and deaths from suicide, alcohol and drugs. Australia is getting materially richer and fairer at work while becoming lonelier and more self-destructive.

7/13 trends significant at p<0.05  (improving 5, worsening 2)

5. Finding 4 — leading the decline, not sharing it

Maybe every rich country is getting lonelier? For each human indicator we place Australia in the OECD distribution of 2010→latest change. Australia's rise in lack of social support (+104%) and deaths of despair (+49%) each outpaced roughly 89% of the OECD — and on deaths of despair the OECD median actually fell. On its weakest dimensions, Australia is a negative outlier moving against the pack.

6. Finding 5 — the blind spot, filled

Of 21 indicators, four are never observed for Australia and four more go dark after 2020 — including life satisfaction, the series that speaks most directly to "Do Australians feel happy?" We fill that one gap with the World Happiness Report: Australians' own life ratings have slid from a 2013 peak of 7.35 to a record-low 6.97 in 2025, 11th in the world. A different survey, the same verdict. The coverage map that follows shows the bias directly — the material series survive, the subjective and relational ones disappear, so any composite flatters Australia by construction.

7. Critical assessment

The verdict is robust; the precise rank is not — "above median" holds in 89% of perturbed worlds, "top third" in 44%. Three independent lenses (relative z-scores, within-country trends, an external absolute scale) converge on the same subjective decline, which is the strength of the evidence. Australia's flagship civic score is a compulsory-voting artefact, not spontaneous engagement. We show Australia is a negative outlier on loneliness and deaths of despair but do not claim to know why — housing stress, service access and measurement change are untested candidates. Because subjective and relational series disappear first, the true deterioration is more likely understated than overstated.

8. Wider context

In 2023 the Australian Treasury launched Measuring What Matters, the nation's first national well-being framework — built on exactly this report's premise, that GDP alone is inadequate. The material strengths echo the enduring "lucky country"; the deterioration we isolate — an OECD-leading rise in loneliness and deaths of despair, a record-low happiness score, chronically stretched housing — is the terrain of Australia's cost-of-living and mental-health debate.

9. Conclusion

Is Australia doing well? Above average — and quietly sliding on the things hardest to count. On the material instruments the 20th century trusted (income, jobs, longevity) Australia is a durable, still-improving success. On the human instruments the 21st century is learning to trust (connection, distress, subjective happiness) it is going backwards faster than almost the entire OECD, and the data pipeline is losing sight of exactly those dimensions. The honest answer to a vague question is not a confident number — it is a stress-tested decomposition that shows where to look.
